# Income Protection — Provider Comparison Guide

> **When to use this file:** Load when client cannot work temporarily (illness or injury) and needs salary continuance. Cross-reference `_recommendation-guide.md` for tax guidance (IP premiums ARE deductible outside super).

---

## Product Overview

Income Protection (IP), also called salary continuance or disability income insurance, pays a monthly benefit (typically 70–75% of pre-disability income) if the insured is unable to work due to illness or injury.

**Key decision factors:**
- Waiting period (30, 60, 90 days, 2 years) — match to client's financial buffer
- Benefit period (2 years, 5 years, to age 65/70) — match to risk horizon
- Definition of disability (own-occupation vs any-occupation)
- Indemnity vs agreed-value (agreed-value no longer available to new customers since 2021)
- Tax deductibility of premiums (outside super: fully deductible)
- Monthly benefit cap (usually 70–75% of income)

---

## Regulatory Context

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Agreed-value IP abolished (2021)** | APRA banned new agreed-value IP policies from April 2021. All new IP is indemnity-value only. |
| **Indemnity-value IP** | Benefit calculated based on actual income at time of claim, not at time of application |
| **Benefit cap** | Generally 70–75% of pre-disability income (some products allow 80% with super offset) |
| **Super + retail IP** | If IP is inside super, benefit is also reduced by super fund IP benefit (anti-overlap rules) |
| **Tax on IP benefit** | IP benefits are taxable income — insurer withholds PAYG tax at claim |
| **IP premiums (outside super)** | Fully tax-deductible as per s8-1 ITAA 1997 |
| **IP premiums (inside super)** | Funded from concessional contributions — no additional personal deduction |

---

## Waiting Period Guide

| Waiting Period | Best For |
|---|---|
| **30 days** | Self-employed with minimal cash buffer, contractor |
| **60 days** | PAYG employee with 2 months savings |
| **90 days** | Employee with 3+ months of sick leave/savings (most common) |
| **2 years** | Client with strong long-term disability need but can self-fund short-term |

> Premium saving: Moving from 30-day to 90-day waiting period typically reduces premium by 20–30%.

---

## Benefit Period Guide

| Benefit Period | Best For |
|---|---|
| **2 years** | Budget-conscious clients; covers most temporary disabilities |
| **5 years** | Middle ground; covers most serious but recoverable conditions |
| **To age 65** | Recommended for most clients (especially those under 50) |
| **To age 70** | Available from some providers for office-based occupations |

> The majority of long-term IP claims last 2–3 years. However, to-age-65 provides peace of mind for permanent disability risk and is the standard recommendation for most working adults.

## Provider Deep-Dive

### TAL Life — Income Protection

**Strengths:**
- **Benefit period to age 70** available for eligible occupations (longest in market)
- Strong rehabilitation support (return-to-work program)
- Partial/proportional disability benefit: if client can work part-time, receives proportional benefit
- Mental health coverage: included (not excluded) — important given rising mental health claims
- Recurrent disability clause: if same condition recurs within 6 months, new waiting period not required

**Weaknesses:**
- Stepped premiums can be expensive at older ages
- Premium level pricing less competitive vs some peers

**Best for:** Clients who want the longest possible benefit period (to age 70), professionals with own-occupation definition need.

---

### AIA Australia — Income Protection

**Strengths:**
- AIA Vitality integration: premium discounts for maintaining health targets
- Strong own-occupation definition for professional occupations
- Comprehensive rehabilitation benefit (vocational and physical)
- Built-in claim indexation: benefit increases by CPI each year during the claim
- **Mental health claims:** No blanket mental health exclusion on standard products

**Weaknesses:**
- Benefit period maximum age 65 (not 70 like TAL)
- Vitality-dependent pricing may not suit all clients

**Best for:** Health-conscious clients, professionals under 50, clients who want ongoing premium cost management through wellness.

---

### Zurich / OnePath — Income Protection

**Strengths:**
- Strong own-occupation definition wording (court-tested language)
- Premium freeze available on some IP products
- **Specific injury benefit:** Automatic payment for listed injuries (e.g. broken leg) without satisfying the full waiting period — immediate income support
- Comprehensive mental health coverage
- Rehabilitation expenses benefit

**Weaknesses:**
- Adviser-channel only
- Benefit period max age 65

**Best for:** Clients valuing definition certainty, those who want specific injury benefit for immediate payment, professionals in premium-sensitivity situations.

---

### MLC Life — Income Protection

**Strengths:**
- Competitive for group/employer-sponsored IP schemes (MLC Plum corporate platform)
- Group IP often cheaper due to pooled underwriting

**Weaknesses:**
- Group IP definition typically any-occupation after 2 years (not own-occupation for full benefit period)
- Individual retail products less competitive than TAL/AIA/Zurich

**Best for:** Clients covered through an MLC-linked employer super/IP scheme.

## Income Protection Inside vs Outside Superannuation

| Factor | Outside Super (Retail) | Inside Super (Group/Fund) |
|---|---|---|
| **Premium tax deduction** | Fully deductible (s8-1 ITAA 1997) | Not personally deductible (paid from concessional contributions at 15% tax) |
| **Benefit tax treatment** | Taxable income (PAYG withheld) | Taxable income |
| **Own-occupation definition** | Available | Limited (any-occ common in super) |
| **Benefit period** | Up to age 70 (retail) | Usually 2 years in super |
| **Waiting period** | 30–90 days (retail choice) | Typically 60–90 days |
| **Cost** | Higher (individual underwriting) | Lower (group rates) |
| **Claims support** | Direct with insurer/adviser | Via trustee |
| **Recommendation** | Complex needs, self-employed, high income | Simple needs, cost-conscious, low risk |

> **Key insight:** Outside-super IP premiums are the ONLY life insurance premium that is tax-deductible. For a client on a 37% marginal tax rate, a $3,000/year IP premium costs effectively $1,890 after tax. This is a significant selling point vs super-funded IP.

## Mental Health and IP — Important Note

Mental health claims are the fastest-growing IP claim category in Australia (per APRA). Key adviser obligations:

1. Do **not** accept blanket mental health exclusion loadings without challenging the insurer's medical basis
2. If insurer applies a mental health exclusion, document the client's full medical history and consider alternative providers
3. **AFCA:** Mental health exclusion disputes are a significant portion of AFCA's insurance caseload — clients have strong grounds to challenge exclusions that are not medically supported

---

## Self-Employed Client Considerations

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Income verification** | Indemnity IP: benefit = actual income at claim time. Self-employed income can fluctuate — document income carefully at application |
| **Business expense cover** | Separate "business expenses" IP policy available to cover fixed business costs (rent, staff wages) during disability — distinct from personal IP |
| **Waiting period** | Self-employed clients often choose 30-day waiting period (no sick leave entitlement) |
| **Definition** | Own-occupation preferred — self-employed can't always pivot to "any" occupation |

---

## Common Exclusions — Income Protection

- Pre-existing conditions (standard exclusion for conditions in the 5 years prior to application)
- Intentional self-injury or suicide attempt
- War and armed conflict
- Criminal activity
- Non-disclosure of health history
- Unemployment (not the same as disability — IP does not cover job loss)
- Normal pregnancy (complications of pregnancy may be covered — varies by policy)
