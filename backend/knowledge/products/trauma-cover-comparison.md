# Trauma / Critical Illness Cover — Provider Comparison Guide

> **When to use this file:** Load when client wants a lump sum on diagnosis of a serious illness (cancer, heart attack, stroke, etc.). Cross-reference `_recommendation-guide.md` for regulatory context (trauma cannot be purchased inside super for new policies post-2014).

---

## Product Overview

Trauma insurance (also called critical illness or CI cover) pays a tax-free lump sum on diagnosis or occurrence of specified medical events. Unlike Income Protection, it is not tied to inability to work — the lump sum is paid on diagnosis regardless of employment status.

**Key decision factors:**
- Number and breadth of covered conditions
- Definition quality for each covered condition (especially cancer, heart attack, stroke)
- Waiting/survival period (typically 14–30 days after diagnosis)
- Buy-back option (restores Life cover after a trauma claim)
- Premium structure (stepped premiums increase steeply after age 40)
- Children's trauma benefit availability

---

## Regulatory Context

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Trauma in super (post-2014)** | **Prohibited** — no new trauma/CI cover can be established inside superannuation after 1 July 2014 (SIS Act condition of release constraint) |
| **Existing trauma in super** | Pre-2014 grandfathered trauma policies in super can be retained but not increased |
| **Tax on lump sum** | Trauma benefit is **tax-free** — it is a capital payment, not income |
| **Premiums** | NOT tax-deductible (premium is private expenditure) |
| **Life Code of Practice (FSC)** | Three mandatory "headline" conditions must be included: **invasive cancer, heart attack, stroke** |
| **Survival period** | Typically 14 days after the qualifying event — claim not paid if client dies within survival period |
| **Waiting period** | Usually 90 days from policy commencement (some events have no waiting period if accidental) |

## Covered Conditions — Market Standard

### FSC Life Code Minimum (All Retail Providers Must Cover)

1. **Invasive Cancer** — malignant tumour, lymphoma, leukaemia (excluding early-stage/non-invasive)
2. **Heart Attack** — confirmed myocardial infarction with specified enzyme/ECG criteria
3. **Stroke** — cerebrovascular accident resulting in permanent neurological deficit

### Standard Conditions (Most Retail Providers)

| Condition Category | Common Events Included |
|---|---|
| **Cardiac** | Heart attack, coronary artery bypass, heart valve surgery, cardiomyopathy, aortic surgery |
| **Neurological** | Stroke, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, motor neurone disease, multiple sclerosis |
| **Cancer** | Invasive cancer, benign brain tumour |
| **Organ** | Kidney failure, liver failure, lung disease (end-stage), aplastic anaemia |
| **Organ transplant** | Major organ transplant (heart, lung, kidney, liver, pancreas) |
| **Other** | Blindness, deafness, loss of limb, loss of speech, burns (severe), paralysis, coma |

## Provider Deep-Dive

### TAL Life — Trauma Cover

**Strengths:**
- 40+ covered conditions — broad standard list
- **Children's trauma benefit:** Built into some policies — covers dependent children up to age 20 for listed conditions at no extra premium
- **Partial trauma benefit:** Some conditions (e.g. early-stage heart attack, less-severe stroke) attract a partial payment (e.g. 25%) rather than full sum insured
- **Buy-back option:** After a trauma claim, TAL allows the client to reinstate their life cover sum insured (so the life policy is not reduced by the trauma payout)
- Rehabilitation benefit: covers cost of rehabilitation following a covered event

**Weaknesses:**
- Cancer definition is standard invasive-only (no automatic early-stage cancer payment in base product)
- Stepped premiums rise significantly after age 45

**Best for:** Clients who want a comprehensive standard list, children's cover inclusion, buy-back feature.

---

### AIA Australia — Trauma Cover

**Strengths:**
- **50+ covered conditions** — largest standard list in market
- **AIA Vitality integration:** Premium discounts apply to trauma cover
- **Early-stage cancer rider** available (adds early-stage cancer, early-stage heart conditions — pays a smaller benefit on early diagnosis)
- Mental health conditions included in some extended products
- Enhanced cancer definitions: some products include ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) and early prostate cancer with specific criteria

**Weaknesses:**
- Vitality-dependent pricing advantage requires client engagement
- Early-stage cancer rider is an add-on cost (not base product)

**Best for:** Clients who want the broadest conditions list, those with family history of cancer (especially early-stage), health-conscious clients on Vitality.

---

### Zurich / OnePath — Trauma Cover

**Strengths:**
- **Court-tested condition definitions** — Zurich's cancer and heart attack definitions have been upheld in Australian courts with favourable client outcomes
- **Premium freeze:** Like their life and TPD products, Zurich offers a premium freeze option (reduces sum insured to hold premium flat)
- Comprehensive cardiac coverage (10+ cardiac events covered)
- Strong rehabilitation and modification benefits (home/vehicle modifications after trauma)

**Weaknesses:**
- Maximum ~43 conditions (fewer than AIA's 50+)
- No Vitality or wellness discount program

**Best for:** Clients valuing definition certainty (especially cancer/cardiac), older clients concerned about premium escalation (premium freeze).

## Trauma vs Income Protection — Interaction

| Scenario | Trauma | Income Protection |
|---|---|---|
| Client diagnosed with cancer but continues working | **Trauma pays** (regardless of work status) | IP does NOT pay (client still earning) |
| Client diagnosed with cancer AND cannot work | **Both pay** | IP pays monthly benefit |
| Client has heart attack, recovers in 3 months | **Trauma pays** (lump sum on event) | IP pays for 3-month recovery |
| Client has stroke, permanently disabled | **Trauma pays** lump sum | IP pays monthly | TPD may also pay if meets definition |

> **Key message:** Trauma and IP are complementary, not competing. Trauma provides an immediate lump sum regardless of work impact; IP provides ongoing income replacement.

## Cancer Definition — What to Watch For

Cancer is the most common trauma claim category. Key definition differences:

| Feature | Best Practice | Problematic |
|---|---|---|
| **Invasive cancer** | Includes all malignant tumours, lymphoma, leukaemia | Excludes specific cancer types |
| **Early-stage cancer** | Covered with partial benefit (some products) | Excluded entirely |
| **Melanoma** | Stage 1B+ included | Melanoma excluded or limited |
| **Prostate cancer** | Included with Gleason score ≥7 criteria | High exclusion threshold |
| **Skin cancer (BCC/SCC)** | Excluded by most (not classed as invasive) | Correctly excluded |
| **CIS (carcinoma in situ)** | Partially covered by some (as partial benefit) | Fully excluded |

## Partial Trauma Benefits

Most providers offer reduced (partial) benefits for less-severe events:

| Event | Typical Partial Payment |
|---|---|
| Angioplasty | 10–25% of sum insured |
| Early-stage heart attack (minor) | 25% |
| Less-severe stroke (no permanent deficit) | 25% |
| CIS / early-stage cancer | 10–25% (if covered) |

This allows clients to receive some payment for significant but not catastrophic events.

---

## Children's Trauma Benefit

Most retail trauma policies include a **children's trauma benefit** either built-in or as a rider:

- Typically covers children aged 2–18 (or to age 20)
- Sum insured: usually $25,000–$50,000 (or a percentage of parent's sum insured)
- Common covered conditions: cancer, heart conditions, cerebral palsy, cystic fibrosis, meningitis, Type 1 diabetes
- **No additional premium** on some policies (built-in feature)

> A children's trauma benefit is a significant emotional and financial protection for families. Always highlight this feature.

## Tax Treatment — Trauma Cover

| Situation | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|
| Trauma lump sum benefit | **Tax-free** (capital payment, not income) |
| Premiums paid personally | **NOT tax-deductible** |
| Trauma inside super (new) | **Not permissible** since 1 July 2014 |
| Trauma inside super (pre-2014 grandfathered) | Benefit taxed as super lump sum (conditions of release must be met) |

---

## Common Exclusions — Trauma Cover

- Pre-existing conditions (in the 5 years before policy commencement)
- Non-invasive cancers (CIS, early-stage skin cancer, superficial bladder)
- Conditions diagnosed within the 90-day waiting period (unless accidental)
- Non-disclosure of medical history
- Self-inflicted injury or attempted suicide within 13 months
- War and armed conflict

---

## Replacement Risk — Trauma Cover

> When replacing trauma cover, specific risks apply:
> 1. **New waiting period:** 90-day waiting period restarts on the new policy
> 2. **New exclusions:** Any conditions that developed since the original policy was issued may be excluded from the new policy
> 3. **Pre-existing condition exclusions:** New insurer reviews current health at replacement — conditions treated or diagnosed since original policy may attract exclusions
> 4. **Post-2014 rule:** Cannot replace super-held trauma with a new super-held trauma policy

**Replacement is highest-risk for:** Clients who have had any health events since their original trauma policy was issued.
